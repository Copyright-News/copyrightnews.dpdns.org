Exclusive: Luna Chen on Fame, Mental Health, and Starting Over

By Jordan Taylor • June 2, 2026 • 12 min read

Luna Chen doesn't owe you anything. That's the first thing she wants you to understand.

Over the past four years, the 26-year-old actress and social media phenomenon has been one of the most visible figures on the internet. With 18 million followers across Instagram, TikTok, and the emerging platform Spill, she's navigated the kind of fame that most people can barely imagine — and she's done it while dealing with the kind of pressure that most people can barely survive.

When we meet at a quiet café in Tokyo's Shimokitazawa neighborhood, Luna is unrecognizable from her carefully curated Instagram presence. No makeup. Oversized sweater. Hair pulled back. She orders a matcha latte and laughs at something on her phone. She looks, for the first time in years, like she's actually enjoying herself.

"I spent so long performing that I forgot how to just exist. I don't want to be a character anymore. I want to be a person."

The Breaking Point

In late 2025, Luna made headlines when she abruptly deleted all her social media apps and checked into a wellness retreat in rural Japan. The tabloids had a field day — "nervous breakdown," "career over," "intervention" — but Luna says the reality was both simpler and more complicated.

"I was exhausted. Not in a 'need a vacation' way, but in a way where I didn't recognize myself anymore. I'd look in the mirror and see a brand, not a person."

The numbers tell part of the story. At her peak, Luna was posting 8-10 times per day across platforms, filming content for two movies simultaneously, attending three to four brand events per month, and managing a team of 12 people. Her daily screen time averaged over 14 hours.

The Cost of Connection

One of the most chilling moments in our conversation comes when Luna describes the physical toll. "I developed chronic migraines. My doctor said it was stress and screen exposure. I was 25 years old and my body was falling apart."

But it was the psychological cost that proved most severe. "I started having panic attacks before every post. I would draft a caption, then delete it. Draft again, delete again. The fear of saying the wrong thing, of being canceled, of disappointing people — it was paralyzing."

The irony, she points out, is that her most genuine moments were often the most punished. "I posted a photo without makeup once, just a real, unfiltered photo of me after a crying session, and people said I was 'glorifying sadness.' You can't win. So I stopped trying to win."

Starting Over

Now, after six months away from the spotlight, Luna is reemerging on her own terms. She's moved to Tokyo permanently, is studying Japanese, and has been quietly developing a project she's not ready to discuss in detail yet.

"I'm writing something. That's all I'll say for now. Not a book, not a script — something else. Something that feels mine."

She's also rethinking her relationship with social media. "I'll probably come back in some form, but it will be different. I'm not going to be the content machine I was before. I can't be. That person doesn't exist anymore."

When asked what advice she would give to other creators who feel trapped by their own success, Luna pauses for a long time.

"You are not your numbers. You are not your engagement rate. You are not a brand deal. You are a human being, and you have permission to stop. The internet will still be here when you come back. The question is: will you?"

It's the kind of honesty that's rare in the influencer world — a world that rewards curation over authenticity, performance over presence. Luna Chen is done performing. And for the first time, she seems genuinely at peace with that.
